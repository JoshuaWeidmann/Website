Welcome! I am a master's student in Politics and Public Administration at the University of Konstanz. My research focuses on whether (1) artificial intelligence can be used to conduct qualitative interviews in large-scale surveys and (2) pro-democratic education can break authoritarian legacies in new democracies.

I am currently a master's student in Politics and Public Administration at the University of Konstanz. I hold an MSc in Political Sociology from the London School of Economics (LSE). In addition, I work as a research assistant for Prof. Michael M. Bechtel at the University of Cologne and for Prof. David Y. Yang at Harvard University.

Work in Progress

  1. "Dialing Up the Empathy: Using AI Chatbots to Conduct Qualitative Interviews in Mass Surveys" (with Michael M. Bechtel, Aaron Cannon, and Michael Hess). [Abstract]

    The availability of artificial intelligence (AI) may enable researchers to study preference formation qualitatively through unstructured question formats that used to be too costly for inclusion in mass surveys. We experimentally assess whether AI chatbots and their empathic capabilities affect answers to sensitive and non-sensitive questions in qualitative conversations. Our design randomly assigns respondents to a series of pre-set, open-ended questions, a weakly empathic chatbot interviewer, or a highly empathetic chatbot. We evaluate chatbot performance with respect to the informational value of survey responses, perceived empathy and rapport, and price per complete. Our applications include political science (vote choice) and consumer research (drivers of purchasing behavior) questions. Since qualitative interviews promise to increase respondentsâ€™ willingness to provide truthful responses to sensitive questions that are particularly prone to social desirability bias, we also investigate attitudes toward abortion and hiring discrimination. The results shed light on whether AI chatbots are a more useful tool to qualitatively explore the determinants of preferences and overcoming social desirability problems compared to using fixed, open-ended questions determined by the researcher.
     
  2. "How Education in New Democracies Breaks Authoritarian Legacies: Evidence from Eastern Germany". [Abstract]

    A core finding about authoritarian regimes is that societies affected by them tend to be more autocratic and intolerant even after the authoritarian regime itself collapsed and countries have democratized. However, new democracies required popular support to survive. I argue that governments can use education to develop authoritarian societies into democratic ones. I use exogenous variation in school enrollment based on children's birth dates in a fuzzy regression discontinuity design to estimate the effects pro-democratic school curricula had on support for democracy in newly democratized Eastern Germany.
